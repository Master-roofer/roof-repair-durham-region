What is Roof Repair? Understanding the Basics

Let's start with what should be obvious but apparently isn't based on the calls we get: roof repair is fixing specific problems with your existing roof rather than replacing the whole thing. Seems simple enough, right?

Here's where it gets interesting. In Durham Region, we see everything from tiny nail pops to massive wind damage from those surprise Ontario storms that roll through. A roof repair might mean replacing a handful of shingles in Pickering after a windstorm, patching a flat roof membrane on a commercial building in Oshawa, or fixing flashing around a chimney in Whitby that's been leaking for who-knows-how-long because the previous owner "didn't notice."

Common Roof Repairs We Handle in Durham Region

The most common repairs we see break down into a few categories:

Shingle repairs make up about 60% of our residential calls. Missing shingles, lifted shingles, cracked shingles ‚Äì usually caused by wind, aging, or improper installation in the first place. (You'd be surprised how many "new" roofs we see with installation issues that show up within a year.)

Flashing repairs are the sneaky ones. Flashing is the metal that seals transitions ‚Äì around chimneys, vents, valleys, where your roof meets a wall. When flashing fails, water gets in, and by the time you notice the water stain on your ceiling, you've already got bigger problems brewing in your attic.

Flat roof repairs are their own beast entirely. Whether it's EPDM, TPO, modified bitumen, or old tar-and-gravel (we still see these in Durham Region), flat roofs fail differently than sloped roofs. Ponding water, seam failures, membrane punctures ‚Äì these need a different approach entirely.

Structural repairs are less common but more serious. Sagging roof decks, rotted rafters, damaged trusses ‚Äì these usually mean someone ignored smaller problems for way too long, and now we're talking about real money.

Types of Roof Damage: Residential & Commercial

Roof damage comes in many forms, and knowing what you're dealing with makes all the difference in getting it fixed properly (and not getting ripped off in the process).

üèòÔ∏è Residential Roof Damage

Wind Damage

Durham Region gets its share of windstorms. We're talking 80-120 km/h gusts that lift shingles, tear them off entirely, or break the seal strips. Wind damage is usually obvious ‚Äì you'll see shingles in your yard or notice lifted edges on your roof. Insurance usually covers wind damage (if you have the right coverage), but you need to document it properly and act reasonably quickly.

Ice Dam Damage

This is the Ontario special. When snow melts and refreezes at your eaves, it creates a dam that forces water back up under your shingles. You'll see icicles (pretty but problematic), ice buildup at the roof edge, and potentially water stains inside. Ice dams damage shingles, underlayment, and can rot your decking if ignored. They're caused by inadequate attic insulation and ventilation ‚Äì fixing the ice dam without fixing the cause just means you'll have the same problem next winter.

Aging and Weathering

Shingles don't last forever. In Durham Region, you're looking at 20-30 years for quality shingles if everything was done right. As they age, you'll see granule loss (the colored coating coming off), curling edges, cracked shingles, and general deterioration. This isn't repairable ‚Äì it's nature taking its course ‚Äì but you can extend your roof's life with proper maintenance.

Flashing Failures

Flashing is supposed to be the impenetrable barrier where different roof surfaces meet. But caulking deteriorates, metal corrodes (especially in Ontario's salty winter environment), and improper installation catches up with you. Chimney flashing, valley flashing, vent pipe flashing ‚Äì these all have different failure patterns, but they all let water in when they fail.

Pest Damage

Raccoons, squirrels, and birds can do surprising damage. We've seen raccoons tear up shingles to get into attics, squirrels chew through fascia and soffit, and woodpeckers create holes in wood trim. Animal damage usually isn't covered by insurance (it's considered preventable), so you're on your own financially.

üè¢ Commercial Flat Roof Damage

Ponding Water

Flat roofs aren't actually flat ‚Äì they should have slight slope for drainage. When they don't (or when drains get clogged), water ponds. Standing water degrades membrane materials, encourages biological growth, and adds weight load. Any water standing more than 48 hours after rain is a problem that needs addressing.

Membrane Punctures and Tears

Commercial roofs get foot traffic from HVAC techs, antenna installers, and maintenance workers. Sharp objects, tools dropped, careless walking ‚Äì all create punctures in the membrane. Small punctures become big problems fast on flat roofs.

Seam Failures

Flat roof membranes are installed in sections that get seamed together. Those seams are the weak point. Whether it's TPO that wasn't heat-welded properly, EPDM with failing adhesive, or modified bitumen with deteriorating seams, this is where flat roofs typically start leaking.

Blow-Offs

Flat roofs in exposed areas can have sections lifted and torn off by high winds. This is more common with mechanically-attached systems versus fully-adhered systems, but we see it with both. It's usually covered by insurance but requires immediate attention to prevent water damage.

‚ö†Ô∏è Critical Point About Commercial Roofs:

Commercial roof repairs aren't DIY territory. The materials are specialized, the installation techniques require training, and the liability issues are significant. If you own a commercial building in Durham Region with roof problems, get a professional assessment. The cost of a bad repair job far exceeds the cost of doing it right the first time.

Repair vs. Replace: Making the Right Call

This is where homeowners get confused and contractors get shady. Let's be straight about when repair makes sense and when you actually need replacement.

When Repair is the Right Answer

Isolated damage: A few missing or damaged shingles from a recent storm? Repair. Localized leak from failed flashing? Repair. Small area of wind damage? Repair. If the damage is limited to one area and the rest of your roof is in decent shape, repair is almost always more cost-effective.

Newer roof with issues: If your roof is under 10 years old and you're having problems, it's likely an installation defect or isolated damage, not widespread failure. Repair it and potentially go after the original installer if it's their fault.

Budget constraints with serviceable roof: If your roof has some years left but needs small fixes, repair buys you time to save for eventual replacement. Just be realistic about how much time you're buying.

When Replacement is Necessary

Widespread damage across the roof: If you're seeing problems in multiple areas, multiple slopes, or extensive granule loss everywhere, you're past the repair stage. Patching 40% of your roof costs almost as much as replacing it and gives you a Frankenstein result.

Age-related failure: When your 25-year-old roof starts having multiple issues, it's trying to tell you something. Repair one spot and another will fail next month. At a certain point, you're just throwing money at a dying roof.

Decking problems: If your roof decking (the plywood or OSB under your shingles) is rotted, sagging, or damaged, you need more than shingle repair. This requires replacement of affected sections minimum, and often indicates widespread problems.

Multiple layers present: If your house already has two layers of shingles (the maximum allowed by code), any new work means stripping everything off. You can't add a third layer, so you're doing a full replacement whether you like it or not.

Durham Region Specific Factors That Affect Your Roof

Durham Region isn't Vancouver. It's not Calgary. It's not even Toronto proper (though we're close). Our weather patterns, building codes, and local conditions create unique roofing challenges.

Lake Ontario Effect

Living near Lake Ontario moderates our temperatures but increases humidity and moisture exposure. Communities closer to the lake (Whitby, Oshawa waterfront) deal with more moisture-related issues. You'll see more biological growth (algae, moss) on north-facing slopes, more condensation issues in attics, and accelerated deterioration of organic materials.

The lake also influences our wind patterns. We get strong west and southwest winds that hammer west-facing roof slopes harder than other directions. If you're in an exposed location near the water, your roof takes more abuse.

Freeze-Thaw Cycles

Ontario's spring and fall are brutal on roofs. We fluctuate above and below freezing repeatedly, causing expansion and contraction cycles that stress materials. Water gets into tiny cracks, freezes, expands, makes the crack bigger. Rinse and repeat. This is why Ontario roofs don't last as long as roofs in more stable climates.

Ice dams are the winter manifestation of this. They form when heat escaping through your roof melts snow, which then refreezes at the cold eaves. The resulting ice dam forces water back under your shingles. Proper attic insulation and ventilation prevent this, but we still see it constantly because builders cut corners and homeowners don't understand the issue.

Snow Load Requirements

Durham Region building codes require roofs to handle specified snow loads (varies by municipality, typically 2.0-2.4 kPa). This affects how your roof should be built and what happens when it's damaged. Heavy snow puts stress on weakened roof structures, and we see more failures after significant snowfalls.

If you're doing any structural roof repair, make sure your contractor knows current snow load requirements. Older homes might not meet current standards, which matters if you're adding anything to the roof or doing major work.

Wind Exposure

Durham Region is relatively flat with agricultural areas, which means less wind protection than heavily-forested regions. We get straight-line winds, lake-effect winds, and occasional tornado warnings. Your roof needs proper nailing patterns and quality installation to handle this.

The most common wind damage we see: lifted shingles, blown-off shingles, and damaged flashings. This is almost always covered by insurance (if you have wind coverage), but you need to document it properly and file claims in a timely manner.

Building Code Updates

Ontario Building Code updates affect repair work differently than new construction, but contractors still need to meet current ventilation requirements, safety standards, and installation practices. If your roof was built before certain code changes, repair work might trigger requirements to bring some aspects up to current code.

For example, if we're doing significant decking replacement on an older roof, we might need to address ventilation deficiencies at the same time. An honest contractor explains this upfront rather than surprising you with it mid-job.

üå°Ô∏è Climate Projection Impact:

Durham Region's climate is changing. We're seeing more extreme weather events, higher winds, more intense rain, and larger temperature swings. Your roof from 2000 was designed for historical weather patterns, but we're not living in that climate anymore. When doing repairs or planning replacement, consider upgrading to higher wind ratings and better underlayment even if it costs slightly more. The next 20 years will be harder on your roof than the last 20.

Emergency Repairs: What to Do When You Have a Leak

It's 11 PM on a Friday. You're in the living room and you hear dripping. You look up and there's a water stain spreading across your ceiling. Now what?

Immediate Actions (First 30 Minutes)

1. Contain the water inside: Put buckets, pots, towels ‚Äì anything ‚Äì under the leak. Move furniture and electronics away from the area. If water is spreading across the ceiling, poke a small hole at the lowest point to let it drain in a controlled location rather than letting it soak the drywall.

2. Protect your stuff: Get anything valuable away from the leak area. Water stains on hardwood floors cost thousands to fix. Water-damaged electronics don't work. Be proactive.

3. Find the source if it's safe: If you can safely access your attic, get up there with a flashlight and trace the water back to where it's coming in. Take photos. Water travels along rafters and underlay before dripping through your ceiling, so the entry point might not be directly above the ceiling leak.

4. Document everything: Take photos and videos of the leak, the damage, the source if you found it, and any outside conditions (is it raining? was there recent snow?). Your insurance will want this.

Short-Term Temporary Fixes (If You Can Safely Access the Roof)

Only do this if conditions are safe. Don't go on your roof in the dark, in rain, in ice, or if you're not comfortable with heights. Falling off your roof is worse than a leak.

Tarp application: If you have a tarp, you can temporarily cover the damaged area. Extend the tarp at least 4 feet beyond the damaged area in all directions. Weight it down with 2x4s or sandbags ‚Äì don't nail through it into your roof (that creates more holes). This is a temporary measure only.

Temporary patching: Roofing cement, duct tape, plastic sheeting ‚Äì these might stop water temporarily but they're not real fixes. Use them to get through the night, not as a long-term solution.

Calling for Professional Emergency Repair

Most roofing companies (including us) offer emergency services. Here's what to expect:

After-hours calls: Emergency service outside business hours typically costs more. This is normal. You're paying for someone to drop everything and come help you immediately.

Emergency vs. urgent: Emergency means "water is actively coming in my house right now." Urgent means "I found damage that needs fixing soon." Be honest about which situation you have. Crying emergency when it's not wastes everyone's time and money.

Temporary weatherproofing: The first emergency visit is usually about stopping active leaks and preventing further damage ‚Äì tarping, basic patches, temporary measures. Permanent repairs typically come later when conditions allow and materials are available.

Documentation requirements: Take photos before the emergency roofer arrives and after their temporary work. You need this for insurance and for planning permanent repairs.

Common Emergency Scenarios

Storm Damage

High winds, hail, fallen branches ‚Äì storm damage is usually obvious and usually covered by insurance (if you have the right coverage). Get temporary repairs done immediately to prevent further damage, document everything, and call your insurance within 24-48 hours.

Ice Dam Breakthrough

Ice dams forcing water under your shingles and into your attic is an Ontario winter tradition. Short-term fix: carefully remove the ice dam (not by hammering on your roof), improve attic ventilation, and plan for permanent fixes in warmer weather. Long-term fix: proper insulation and ventilation.

Flashing Failure

Flashing leaks often show up after heavy rain or ice melting. They might not seem like emergencies but water is getting into your walls and roof structure. Get it checked quickly. What seems like a small leak can cause expensive hidden damage.

Animal Damage

Raccoons and squirrels can tear up a roof in one night. If you discover animal entry points, get them sealed immediately before they invite their friends. Temporary repairs might just need wire mesh and some blocking until proper roof repair can be done.

‚ö†Ô∏è Safety Warning:

Wet roofs are dangerously slippery. Icy roofs are death traps. Steep roofs are risky even in good conditions. If you're not comfortable, trained, and equipped for roof work, stay off your roof and call professionals. We've seen homeowners hurt themselves trying to save money on emergency repairs. Hospital bills cost more than roofing bills.

After the Emergency

Once immediate damage is controlled:

Schedule permanent repairs: Temporary fixes aren't permanent. Get quotes for proper repair work and schedule it as soon as weather allows.

File insurance claims promptly: Most policies require "prompt" notification. Don't wait months to file a claim for storm damage.

Address underlying causes: If ice dams caused your leak, you need better insulation and ventilation. If wind damage happened, you might need better shingle quality or installation. Fix the cause, not just the symptom.

Check for hidden damage: Water that got into your attic might have damaged insulation, wood framing, or electrical systems. Have these inspected even if the ceiling leak seemed minor.

Prevention: Stop Problems Before They Start

The best roof repair is the one you don't need because you caught problems early. Here's how to avoid expensive surprises.

Annual Roof Inspections

You should inspect your roof twice a year ‚Äì spring and fall. This doesn't mean climbing on your roof (unless you're comfortable and equipped to do so). Here's what to check from the ground and from inside:

From the Ground (with Binoculars)

  • Missing or damaged shingles
  • Lifted shingle edges or corners
  • Excessive granule loss (shiny spots where the colored coating is gone)
  • Sagging ridge lines or roof planes
  • Damaged or missing flashing around chimneys and vents
  • Debris accumulation in valleys
  • Biological growth (moss, algae) on shingles

From Inside the Attic

  • Water stains on underside of decking
  • Daylight visible through roof boards
  • Moisture on insulation or rafters
  • Mold or mildew growth
  • Damaged or inadequate ventilation
  • Evidence of animal entry

Gutter Maintenance

Gutters aren't technically part of your roof, but they protect it. Clogged gutters cause water backup, ice dams, fascia rot, and foundation problems. Clean them twice a year minimum (spring and fall), or install quality gutter guards if you're tired of ladder time.

While you're at it, check that gutters are properly pitched for drainage, downspouts direct water away from your foundation, and no sections are pulling away from the fascia. Loose gutters eventually damage fascia boards, which leads to more expensive repairs.

Tree Management

Trees overhanging your roof drop leaves (clogging gutters), provide shade (encouraging moss and algae), and drop branches in storms (punching holes in your roof). Trim branches back at least 6 feet from your roof. Yes, you lose shade. But you gain roof longevity and fewer repair calls.

Attic Ventilation and Insulation

Proper attic ventilation and insulation prevent ice dams, reduce energy costs, and extend your roof's life. Here's what you need:

Ventilation: Soffit vents at the eaves + ridge vents or gable vents at the peak = air circulation that removes moisture and heat. Without this, you get condensation issues in winter and excessive heat in summer. Both damage your roof from below.

Insulation: Adequate attic insulation keeps heat in your house instead of melting snow on your roof (which causes ice dams). Ontario building code requires R-60 in attics. Many older homes have R-20 or less. Adding insulation pays for itself in energy savings and prevents winter damage.

Air sealing: Before adding insulation, seal air leaks where conditioned air escapes into your attic. Leaks around plumbing penetrations, pot lights, attic hatches ‚Äì seal these first, then insulate.

Flashing Inspections

Flashing is your roof's defense against water intrusion at vulnerable points. Once a year, check:

  • Chimney flashing: Look for gaps, cracked caulking, lifted counter-flashing
  • Vent pipe flashing: Rubber boots crack and deteriorate over time
  • Valley flashing: Check for rust, holes, or separation from shingles
  • Skylight flashing: Common leak points ‚Äì check all around the frame
  • Wall flashing: Where roof meets siding or walls

Small flashing issues caught early cost $200-400 to fix. Ignored until they cause interior water damage? You're looking at thousands.

Prompt Small Repairs

One missing shingle costs $50-100 to replace. Ignore it, and water gets under the remaining shingles, damages the underlayment, rots the decking, and creates interior damage. Now you're at $1,500+. See the pattern?

Don't put off small repairs hoping they'll magically fix themselves. They won't. They'll get worse and more expensive. This is the roofing version of that check engine light you keep ignoring.

Professional Inspections

Every 3-5 years, pay a qualified roofer for a professional inspection. We see things you won't notice from the ground. A professional inspection costs $200-400 and can identify problems before they become emergencies.

If your roof is approaching 15 years old, increase inspection frequency to every 2-3 years. You're entering the age where problems accelerate.

üõ†Ô∏è Maintenance Tip:

Create a roof maintenance checklist and actually follow it. Spring inspection (April/May): check for winter damage, clean gutters, trim trees. Fall inspection (October/November): remove leaves, check flashing, prepare for winter. It's two afternoons a year that can prevent thousands in repair costs. Most homeowners don't do this. Be smarter than most homeowners.

What About Roof Coatings?

You'll see products advertised to "extend your roof's life" or "seal your roof" ‚Äì rubber coatings, acrylic coatings, elastomeric coatings. The reality:

For residential shingle roofs: Generally a waste of money. These products don't significantly extend shingle life and can void warranties. Save your money.

For flat commercial roofs: Coatings can be effective as part of a maintenance program, but they're not magic. They work best on roofs in decent condition as preventive maintenance, not as a band-aid on failing roofs.

Don't let anyone sell you roof coating as a replacement alternative when you actually need replacement. It's not.

Choosing a Contractor: Red Flags and Green Lights

The internet is full of "how to choose a roofing contractor" articles. Most are written by people who've never hired a roofer. Here's what actually matters based on 20+ years in the business.

üö© Red Flags (Run Away)

Door-to-Door After Storms

Storm chasers follow weather patterns, knock on doors, pressure homeowners into immediate contracts, do substandard work, and disappear. They're not local, they're not accountable, and they're not coming back to fix problems. Yes, legitimate local companies also do storm damage work. But if someone shows up at your door unsolicited after a storm, be very suspicious.

Requires Large Deposit or Full Payment Upfront

Legitimate companies don't need 50-100% of the money before they start work. They have credit with suppliers, they have cash flow, they're not depending on your deposit to fund their business operations. Normal is 25-30% deposit maximum, with balance due on completion. Anyone demanding more is either financially unstable or running a scam.

No Physical Address or Local Phone Number

Google Voice numbers, PO boxes, "we'll come to you" ‚Äì these are contractor-speak for "we're not really a business and you can't find us when something goes wrong." Legitimate companies have offices, local phone numbers, and actual addresses you can visit.

No Insurance or WSIB Certificates

In Ontario, roofing contractors must carry liability insurance and WSIB coverage. If someone falls off your roof and they don't have coverage, you're liable. Always verify insurance certificates and WSIB clearance before anyone touches your roof. No exceptions.

Cash-Only, No Written Contract

Working With Insurance Claims

Insurance claims for roof damage can be straightforward or they can be an absolute nightmare. Here's how to navigate the process without losing your mind (or your claim).

What's Typically Covered

Wind damage: If wind lifted or removed shingles, damaged flashing, or caused impact damage (flying debris, fallen branches), this is usually covered. You need to prove it was wind, not aging or poor maintenance.

Hail damage: Hail causes distinct damage patterns on shingles ‚Äì bruising, dimpling, granule loss in specific patterns. Insurance typically covers this, but you need to document it quickly before evidence disappears.

Fallen trees or branches: If a tree or branch falls on your roof due to wind or storm, coverage usually applies. If the tree was dead or obviously hazardous and you didn't remove it, insurance might argue you were negligent.

Ice dam damage: Maybe covered, maybe not. Depends on your policy and whether the insurance company can argue it was caused by inadequate maintenance or ventilation.

What's Usually NOT Covered

Wear and tear: Your 25-year-old roof failing because it's old? Not covered. Insurance covers sudden events, not aging.

Poor maintenance: If damage resulted from lack of maintenance (clogged gutters, ignored small leaks, deferred repairs), insurance will deny your claim.

Installation defects: If your roof was installed incorrectly and problems result, that's between you and the installer, not your insurance company.

Intentional damage or neglect: You can't ignore a problem for years and then claim it suddenly happened.

The Claims Process

Step 1: Document Everything

Before touching anything, take extensive photos and videos: damaged areas from multiple angles, overall roof condition, ground-level debris, comparison photos of undamaged areas, date-stamped images, and interior damage if water got in.

Note the date and time of the storm or event. Weather reports backing up your claim help.

Step 2: Mitigate Further Damage

Your policy requires you to prevent additional damage. Tarp damaged areas, contain water leaks, but don't do permanent repairs until the insurance adjusts it. Keep receipts for emergency mitigation costs ‚Äì often reimbursable.

Step 3: Contact Your Insurance Company

Call promptly. Most policies require "reasonable" notification timing. A few days is fine. Six months is not.

Be honest about what happened. Don't exaggerate. Don't speculate. Stick to facts.

Step 4: The Adjuster Visit

The insurance adjuster inspects your roof and determines what damage is covered. You (or your contractor) should be present for this inspection. What they don't see, they won't approve.

The adjuster writes a report and estimate. This becomes the basis for your claim payment.

Step 5: Review the Estimate

Insurance estimates are often lower than contractor quotes. This is normal. Insurance uses standardized pricing that might not match current local labor rates or account for your specific roof complexity.

Have your contractor review the insurance estimate. Missing items? Incorrect scope? Your contractor can submit a supplement showing what the insurance missed.

Step 6: The Supplemental Process

Contractors find additional damage once work starts (especially hidden damage under shingles or in decking). Document this with photos and submit to insurance for supplemental payment. This is normal and expected.

Step 7: Payment

Insurance typically pays in two stages: ACV (Actual Cash Value) initially, then RCV (Replacement Cost Value) after work is completed. The difference is depreciation. You get full replacement cost only after proving you completed the work.

If you have a mortgage, your lender might be listed on the check. You'll need to deal with their requirements for releasing funds.

Common Insurance Claim Problems

Lowball Estimates

Insurance estimates might be 30-40% lower than actual costs. Don't accept this without pushback. Your contractor's detailed quote showing missing items and current pricing is your leverage.

Denied Claims

If your claim is denied, ask why in writing. You can appeal. Sometimes getting your contractor or a public adjuster involved helps. Don't accept initial denials without understanding the reasoning.

Scope Disputes

Insurance wants to pay for minimal work. You (and your contractor) know more work is needed. This leads to negotiations. Having detailed documentation, expert opinions, and firm communication helps.

Matching Issues

Your shingles are discontinued. Insurance won't pay to replace the entire roof for one damaged section. You end up with mismatched repairs. This is frustrating but common. Document it so future buyers know the situation.

Should You Use a Public Adjuster?

Public adjusters work for you, not the insurance company. They typically charge 5-10% of your claim settlement. For complex claims, large damage, or denied claims, they can be worth it. For straightforward claims, probably not necessary.

Working with Your Contractor

Good contractors help with insurance claims: meeting adjusters, documenting damage, submitting supplements, providing detailed estimates. We do this regularly. Bad contractors tell you to "sign over your claim" or "let us handle everything" ‚Äì red flags.

Your contractor can advocate for appropriate repair scope and costs, but ultimately it's your claim and your responsibility to communicate with your insurance company.

Never let a contractor tell you to "upgrade" things beyond what's needed and inflate the insurance claim. Never allow damage to be exaggerated. Never file false claims. Insurance fraud is illegal, it's prosecuted, and it affects everyone's premiums. Be honest. The system works when people play by the rules.

DIY vs. Professional: When to Call the Experts

YouTube makes everything look easy. But roof work is dangerous, technical, and unforgiving of mistakes. Here's the honest assessment of what you can DIY and what you absolutely shouldn't.

DIY-Appropriate Tasks (If You're Comfortable and Careful)

Basic Gutter Cleaning

Using a ladder safely? You can clean gutters. It's messy but straightforward. Just use proper ladder technique, have someone spot you, and don't overreach.

Ground-Level Inspections

Walking around your house with binoculars looking for problems? Definitely DIY territory. Take photos, make notes, show them to a professional.

Interior Attic Inspections

If you can safely access your attic, checking for water stains, ventilation issues, and insulation condition is reasonable DIY work. Just watch where you step ‚Äì only walk on framing, not between joists where you'll fall through the ceiling.

Minor Caulking and Sealant Application

Small gaps around vent pipes, tiny spots where flashing meets wall ‚Äì if it's ground-level or easily accessible, you can handle basic caulking. Use proper roof sealant (not regular caulk), apply it correctly, and understand it's temporary not permanent.

Professional-Only Territory (Don't Even Think About DIY)

Any Work on Steep or High Roofs

Roofs over 6/12 pitch (6" rise per 12" run) require safety equipment and training. Three-story homes? Four-story townhouses? Leave it to professionals with proper equipment. Falling is not worth saving money.

Shingle Replacement

Looks simple on YouTube. Actually requires: matching shingles, proper nailing patterns, correct installation techniques, understanding of underlayment, and avoiding creating new problems. DIY shingle repairs often cause more damage than they fix. Plus, if you mess it up, your insurance and warranty are compromised.

Flashing Work

Flashing installation is where most leaks originate. It requires understanding of water flow, proper materials, correct installation sequences, and integration with surrounding materials. This is not DIY-friendly even if you're handy.

Structural Repairs

Rotted decking, damaged trusses, sagging structures ‚Äì these require construction knowledge, building code compliance, and engineering in some cases. Absolutely hire professionals.

Flat Roof Repairs

Flat roofs use specialized materials (EPDM, TPO, modified bitumen) that require specific tools, techniques, and training. Heat welding TPO? Torch application of modified bitumen? These aren't DIY activities unless you want to burn your building down or create a leak factory.

Working With Ice Dams

People die trying to remove ice dams. They fall off ladders, get hit by falling ice, damage their roofs with axes and hammers, or electrocute themselves with heating cables. If you have an ice dam problem, call professionals or wait for warmer weather.

Working conditions: Snow, ice, short daylight hours ‚Äì these make winter roofing difficult and dangerous. Emergency repairs happen, but non-emergency work waits for better weather.

What can be done: Emergency leak repairs, tarping, temporary weatherproofing. Flat roof repairs using certain materials can work in winter. But most shingle work waits for spring.

A: It comes down to extent of damage, age of roof, and cost-effectiveness. If damage is localized (one area, one slope, specific incident) and your roof is under 20 years old, repair usually makes sense. If you're seeing problems in multiple areas, widespread deterioration, or your roof is past 20 years with multiple issues, replacement is likely more economical. Get a professional assessment ‚Äì honest contractors will tell you when repair is sufficient even though replacement would make them more money. A: Quality repairs on roofs in decent condition: 5-10 years minimum, potentially the remaining life of your roof. Flashing repairs: 10-15 years if done properly.
